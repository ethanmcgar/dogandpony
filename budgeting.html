Budgeting

Know where your money goes. Simple frameworks and habits so you can spend on what matters without the guilt—or the surprise overdrafts.

Why budget?

A budget isn’t about restriction—it’s about clarity. When you know how much you earn and where it goes, you can choose to save, pay down debt, or spend on things you value instead of wondering where the money went.

The 50/30/20 rule

One of the simplest starting points: split your take-home pay into three buckets.

  • 50% needs — Rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments. Non-negotiable basics.
  • 30% wants — Dining out, streaming, hobbies, new clothes. The stuff that makes life enjoyable.
  • 20% savings and debt payoff — Emergency fund, retirement, extra payments on debt. Pay yourself first.

The exact percentages can flex (e.g. high rent cities might be 60/20/20). The idea is to name the buckets and give each a job.

No shame in starting If 50/30/20 feels impossible right now, start by tracking for one month. Just see where the money goes. Then set one small goal—e.g. “save 5%” or “cap dining at $X.”

Zero-based budgeting

Every dollar gets a job before the month starts. Income minus all planned expenses and savings equals zero. Nothing is “leftover” by accident—you decide in advance. Tools like a simple spreadsheet or apps (YNAB, etc.) can help, but a pen and paper work too.

How to track spending

You don’t need a fancy app to start. Options that work:

  • Bank and card statements — Download or review once a month. Categorize and add up.
  • Spreadsheet — One column for category, one for planned, one for actual. Compare.
  • Automated apps — Link accounts and let the app categorize (check privacy and security).

Consistency beats perfection. Even a rough picture of spending will reveal patterns and leaks.

Common pitfalls

Treating a budget as a one-time plan instead of something you revisit. Forgetting irregular expenses (annual fees, car registration). Or being so strict that you quit after two weeks. Start simple, review monthly, and adjust as life changes.
