Retirement & insurance

401(k)s, IRAs, and the types of insurance that actually protect your future—without the fear-mongering.

Retirement accounts: the basics

Retirement accounts are tax-advantaged: you either get a break when you contribute (traditional) or when you withdraw (Roth). The earlier you start, the more compound growth you get. Even small, regular contributions add up over decades.

401(k) and similar workplace plans

If your employer offers a 401(k), 403(b), or similar plan, contribute at least enough to get the full employer match if there is one. That’s free money. The money goes in before taxes (traditional) or after taxes (Roth, if your plan offers it), grows tax-deferred, and you pay taxes on withdrawal in retirement (traditional) or take tax-free withdrawals (Roth). There are annual contribution limits; the IRS updates them each year.

IRAs

An Individual Retirement Account (IRA) is something you open on your own, not through work. Traditional IRA: contributions may be tax-deductible; growth is tax-deferred; you pay tax when you withdraw. Roth IRA: you contribute after-tax money; growth and qualified withdrawals are tax-free. Income limits apply for deductibility (traditional) and contribution eligibility (Roth). IRAs are useful if you don’t have a workplace plan or want to save more than the 401(k) limit.

Order of operations First get the 401(k) match, then max a Roth or traditional IRA if you can, then put more in the 401(k). Adjust based on your tax situation and goals.

Insurance: what most people need

Insurance transfers risk. You pay a premium so that if something bad happens, the policy helps cover the cost. The goal is to protect against losses you can’t afford—not to insure every small risk.

  • Health insurance — Essential. Medical bills can be catastrophic. Get it through work, the marketplace, or a public program if you qualify.
  • Renters or homeowners — Protects your stuff and liability. Often required by landlords or lenders.
  • Auto — Required by law in most places. At least carry liability; consider collision/comprehensive if the car has real value.
  • Life insurance — Important if someone depends on your income (spouse, kids). Term life is simple and usually enough; avoid complex products sold as investments unless you understand them.
  • Disability — Replaces income if you can’t work. Some employers offer it; otherwise it’s worth considering if your family relies on your paycheck.

What to skip (or be skeptical of)

Extended warranties, some types of life insurance that mix investing with insurance, and niche policies for small risks are often poor value. Read the terms, compare to the cost of just saving for a rainy day, and avoid high-pressure sales tactics—that’s the real dog and pony show.
