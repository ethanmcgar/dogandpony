Saving & emergency funds

Build a safety net first. Why 3–6 months of expenses matters, where to keep the money, and how to pay yourself before the world gets a cut.

Why an emergency fund first?

Life throws curveballs: job loss, medical bills, car repairs, a broken appliance. Without cash set aside, people lean on credit cards or loans—and interest turns a bad month into a long-term problem. An emergency fund is your buffer so you don’t have to borrow when things go wrong.

How much to save

The standard rule is 3–6 months of essential expenses (rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments). Use 3 months if your income is stable and you have backup (e.g. a partner’s income); aim for 6 if your work is irregular or you’re the sole earner. Some people save up to a year for extra peace of mind.

Start small Even $500–$1,000 can cover many small emergencies and reduce the need to put them on a card. Build from there toward your full target.

Where to keep it

Emergency money should be liquid (easy to get) and safe. A high-yield savings account (HYSA) is the usual choice: you earn more interest than a typical bank account, and your money is FDIC-insured. Keep it in a separate account so you’re not tempted to dip in for non-emergencies.

High-yield savings

Traditional brick-and-mortar banks often pay very low interest. Online and some smaller banks offer HYSAs with much better rates. Compare APY (annual percentage yield)—that’s the rate that includes compounding. There’s no need to chase the absolute highest; pick a reputable institution and move on.

Pay yourself first

Instead of saving “whatever’s left,” set up automatic transfers on payday. Send a fixed amount (or percentage) to savings before you pay bills or spend. You’ll adapt to what’s left; if you wait until the end of the month, there’s often nothing left to save.

After the emergency fund

Once you hit your 3–6 month target, you can redirect that monthly savings toward other goals: retirement accounts, a down payment, or debt payoff. Keep the emergency fund in place and only tap it for real emergencies—then refill it as soon as you can.
