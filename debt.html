Debt

Good debt vs. bad debt, payoff strategies that work, and how to stop the interest from running the show.

Good debt vs. bad debt

Not all debt is equal. The distinction isn’t moral—it’s about whether the debt helps you build assets or just drains you.

  • Often “good” (or at least acceptable) — Mortgages (you build equity), sometimes student loans (if they lead to higher earning potential), and occasionally low-rate business loans. The key is manageable payments and a clear path to payoff or an asset that grows.
  • Usually “bad” — High-interest credit card debt, payday loans, and debt for things that lose value (e.g. vacations, depreciating cars) with no plan to pay it off quickly. The interest compounds against you.

“Good” debt can still become bad if you borrow more than you can handle. The goal is to minimize high-interest debt and pay it off as fast as you can.

Avalanche vs. snowball

Two popular ways to tackle multiple debts:

  • Avalanche — Pay minimums on everything, then put extra money toward the debt with the highest interest rate. Mathematically, this saves the most interest over time.
  • Snowball — Pay minimums on everything, then put extra toward the smallest balance first. When that’s paid off, roll that payment into the next smallest. You get psychological wins (fewer accounts, quick progress) that keep many people motivated.

Both work. If you’re motivated by numbers, choose avalanche. If you need quick wins to stay on track, snowball is valid. The real enemy is inaction.

Minimums are non-negotiable Never skip minimum payments to put more toward another debt. Late or missed payments hurt your credit and can trigger fees and rate increases.

Understanding interest

Interest is the cost of borrowing. With credit cards it’s usually quoted as an APR (annual percentage rate), but it’s often applied daily. Carrying a balance means you pay interest on the previous balance plus new purchases—so debt can grow fast. The faster you pay down the principal, the less interest you pay.

When to consider consolidation or refinancing

If you have multiple high-rate cards, a balance transfer to a 0% APR card or a low-rate consolidation loan can reduce interest and simplify payments. Read the fine print: balance transfers often have fees and the 0% is temporary. Refinancing student or other loans can lower your rate but may extend the term—run the numbers on total interest, not just the monthly payment.
