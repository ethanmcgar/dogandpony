Investing basics

Index funds, compound interest, and why time in the market beats timing the market. No stock-picking or get-rich-quick shows required.

Why invest?

Savings accounts keep your money safe but earn little. Over decades, inflation erodes that purchasing power. Investing—owning a slice of companies or the broader market—gives your money a chance to grow faster than inflation and build long-term wealth. The tradeoff is short-term volatility: the value can go down before it goes up.

Compound interest

Compound interest means you earn returns on your initial investment and on the returns you’ve already earned. Over time, that creates exponential growth. Starting early matters more than picking the perfect moment: an extra decade in the market can mean a much larger nest egg.

Time in the market vs. timing the market

Nobody consistently predicts when the market will rise or fall. Trying to “time” entries and exits usually leads to worse results than staying invested. The strategy that works for most people: invest regularly (e.g. each paycheck), hold through downturns, and let time do the work. Boring beats clever.

Start with index funds Index funds track a whole market (e.g. S&P 500 or total U.S. stock market). They’re diversified, low-cost, and don’t require you to pick individual stocks. For most people, they’re the best default.

Stocks vs. bonds (simplified)

Stocks (equities) represent ownership in companies. They tend to have higher long-term return potential and higher short-term risk. Bonds are loans to governments or companies; they typically pay interest and are less volatile than stocks. A mix of both can smooth out bumps. Target-date funds and balanced funds do this mixing for you based on your expected retirement year or risk tolerance.

Where to invest

Use tax-advantaged accounts first: a 401(k) or similar through work (especially if there’s an employer match—that’s free money), and an IRA. Once you’re saving enough there, a taxable brokerage account is the next step. Choose low-cost brokers and avoid products with high fees or flashy promises.

Diversification and risk

Don’t put all your money in one company or one sector. Index funds spread your money across hundreds or thousands of holdings, which reduces the impact of any single failure. Risk is real—markets can drop 20% or more in a bad year—but over long periods, broad market investing has historically rewarded patient investors.
