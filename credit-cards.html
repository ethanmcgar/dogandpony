Credit cards & credit scores

Your score affects loans, rates, and even rentals. Here’s what actually moves it—and how to use credit without letting it use you.

What is a credit score?

A credit score is a number (usually 300–850) that summarizes how likely you are to repay debt. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve you and at what interest rate. The two main models in the U.S. are FICO and VantageScore; they use similar factors but weight them slightly differently.

What actually moves your score?

Five main factors drive your score:

  • Payment history — Paying on time is the biggest factor. One late payment can hurt; defaults and collections hurt a lot.
  • Amounts owed (utilization) — How much you owe vs. your total credit limits. Keeping utilization under 30% (and lower is better) helps.
  • Length of credit history — Older accounts help. Don’t close old cards just to “clean up” if they have no fee.
  • Credit mix — Having both revolving (cards) and installment (loans) can help slightly.
  • New credit — Applying for many accounts in a short time can lower your score temporarily.
Quick win Pay your statement balance in full by the due date. You avoid interest and build a strong payment history. Carrying a balance does not improve your score.

How to check your credit for free

You’re entitled to free reports from each of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) once per year at annualcreditreport.com. Many banks and card issuers also offer free score and report access. Checking your own credit is a “soft” inquiry and does not hurt your score.

Using credit cards wisely

Credit cards are a tool: they can build credit and offer rewards, or they can trap you in high-interest debt if you spend more than you can pay.

  • Use the card for planned spending you’ll pay off each month.
  • Set up autopay for at least the minimum (and ideally the full balance).
  • Prefer cards with no annual fee unless rewards clearly outweigh the cost.
  • Ignore “preapproved” offers that sound too good—they’re often the dog and pony show. Read the terms.

APR and fine print

APR (annual percentage rate) is the cost of borrowing on the card. If you pay in full every month, you pay 0% interest. If you carry a balance, interest compounds—often daily. Introductory 0% APR can help with a planned purchase or payoff, but know when the rate jumps and what the go-to rate will be.
